Communication & Professionalism

10–15 min
In an online course, your words are your presence. How you communicate with your instructor and classmates shapes how they perceive you — and can directly affect the help and flexibility you receive.

Emailing your instructor — what works and what doesn't

New message
To professor.smith@yourcollege.edu
Subject Question about Essay 2 rubric — ENG 101, Section 04
Dear Professor Smith,

I am working on Essay 2 and have a question about the rubric. The criteria mention "analytical depth" — could you help me understand what that looks like in practice? I have a draft started and want to make sure I am on the right track before I finish.

I know you mentioned office hours on Thursdays at 2pm — would that be a good time to discuss this, or would you prefer I send my draft first?

Thank you for your time.

Jordan Rivera
ENG 101, Section 04 | Student ID: 123456
Subject line includes course name, section, and a specific topic — instructor knows exactly what this is before opening it
Greeting uses the instructor's correct title and name
One clear, specific question — not a vague "I'm confused"
Shows the student has already started working — not asking for a shortcut
Closes with full name, course, and student ID — easy to look up
New message
To professor.smith@yourcollege.edu
Subject question
hey,

i missed class and was wondering what we did. also i dont really get the essay. when is it due? will there be an extension?

thanks
Subject line "question" tells the instructor nothing — this may sit unread for days
No greeting, no name — reads as dismissive even if that wasn't the intent
Asking "what did we miss" signals the student hasn't checked announcements or Canvas first
Asking for an extension with no explanation or context — unlikely to go well
No name, no course, no section — instructor has no way to identify this student
Allow 24–48 hours for a reply before following up. Most instructors respond within one business day — but evenings, weekends, and holidays are not business days.

Discussion board etiquette

Do this
Read all existing posts before replying so you don't repeat what's already been said
Quote or reference the specific point you're responding to
Disagree with ideas respectfully — challenge the argument, not the person
Add something new — a question, an example, a different angle
Post early enough that classmates can respond before the deadline
Avoid this
"Great post, I agree!" responses with no substance — they don't earn participation credit
Typing in all lowercase or using texting abbreviations in academic discussions
Posting your initial response at 11:55pm the night it's due — no one can reply
Dismissing classmates' perspectives without explanation
Sharing personal information about others without consent

Policies every online student needs to understand

Academic integrity
Submitting work that isn't yours — including copying from classmates, paraphrasing sources without citation, or purchasing papers — is a violation with serious consequences. When in doubt, cite it.
AI use policies
Each instructor sets their own AI policy — some prohibit it entirely, some allow it with disclosure, some welcome it. Check your syllabus. "I didn't know" is not a defense after the fact.
Respectful communication
Online courses are subject to your institution's student code of conduct. Hostile, harassing, or discriminatory language in discussions or emails can result in removal from the course.
Netiquette
Tone is hard to read in text. Before posting, re-read your message and ask: "Would I say this out loud in a classroom?" If it could be misread as aggressive or sarcastic, revise it.
